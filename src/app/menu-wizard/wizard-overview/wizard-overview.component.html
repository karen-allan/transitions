Planning Ahead Made Easier!

Below are the major end-of-life care plan documents that you likely no nothing about, but are crucial to your future well-being. You can read more about what each form entails and then decide which ones, if not all, are a priority for you. They are listed in the order that you would likely need them depending on your age and health.
∞ You are under 65 and in good health and fitness:
If you consider that you are still physically well you will probably want life saving treatments and procedures in case you land in the hospital due to accident or injury. Nevertheless, just as you should have life insurance for your dependents even though you may never use it, you should consider any that life situations can change in an instant. Because of this it is important put some things in writing so that your wishes are known to your family and friends.

Let's say you are in a car accident and unable to speak for yourself. When you arrive at the hospital - a social worker or other health professional will fill out a form called a Temporary Substitute Decision Maker that they are legally required to follow in the order given on the form. The order is:

Spouse > Adult Children if any > Parents > Siblings > Grandparents > Grandchildren > Others Related by Birth or Adoption > Close Friend(s) and Others related by marriage. There is also a place to enter one or two people who you DO NOT wish to have any say over your health or well-being (although I don't know how they would know how to fill that one out!).

To read more about the TSDM form in detail click here.

If you have no issues with the idea that your health and life decisions are about to be made by someone selected by a stranger, then this might work for you. If, however you know, for instance, that you want your oldest grand-child who is over 19 to be the decision maker for you, but you have not written this wish down, it is highly unlikely that they will miraculously work their way down the list to that level. Maybe instead, they will choose your spouse, the top person on the list to call. But they may be an ex-spouse and may not have your best interests at heart.

It is for this reason, that it is smart and proactive to fill out a Representative Section 9 form to legally and permanently state who you would prefer to make life and death decisions on your behalf.

And by following this Representative Section-9 page you can quickly and easily select the one or, at maximum two people you trust who would best handle any health related decisions if you are not in a position to do so due to accident or injury. By filling out this list, you are letting the health care team know who you have specifically chosen to make decisions for you related to your situation.
∞ You have one or more chronic conditions, at any age:
Common chronic health conditions include:
  • Diabetes
  • High Blood Pressure
  • Kidney Disease
  • Heart Disease
  • Lung Disease
  • Liver Disease
  • Parkinson’s
  • ALS
  • Huntington’s
  • Multiple Sclerosis
  • Vascular Disease
  • Auto-immune Disorders
  • Early to Moderate Dementia
If you have these or any other chronic condition it is very important to put a plan together for your family to follow. In addition to the Representative Section 9 document listed above you are wise to create an Advanced Care Plan or Advance Directive.
∞ You have a life-threatening illness and your life-expectancy is less than one to two years, at any age:
If you have been told some kind of time limit to your life, it is imperative to make an end of life plan. The plan can be as simple or as detailed as you like and can include DNR orders, EDITH plans (Expected Death in the Home) funeral arrangements, celebrations etc.

If you want to ensure that your needs and wishes are respected right to the end, you should seriously consider making an Advanced Care Plan. Although the Representative Section 9 form has a few lines where you can enter more detailed wishes, an Advanced Care Plan aka Advanced Directive allows you to consider a myriad of questions and concerns to address earlier rather than later.
∞ You are over 65 and relatively healthy:
Even if you are still healthy and feeling fine, getting your affairs in order at this age, is essential to the peace of mind of yourself and your family.

After 65 years of age, it is assumed that, no matter your apparent health, you are more vulnerable and less resilient to disease and injury. Advance Care Planning should be an urgent call to action at this point in your life.
∞ You are over 65 and having trouble managing your day-to-day affairs and the activities of daily living.:
If you are a “frail elder” meaning you need help with your daily care, meals and house-keeping then it is even more essential to create an advanced care plan to put on your fridge door or somewhere it can be easily found by family and friends.

As a frail elder, you need the above forms filled out along with ensuring that you or your family have investigated care homes or have more services brought to your home, such as Meals on Wheels. You may also consider filling out the following forms.

In this situation along with the above you may want to fill out a DNR (do not resuscitate) form. This tells Paramedics and Hospital staff that you do not wish to be revived through Artificial Respiration methods. Read here why CPR may not be for you.

You may also want to fill out an EDITH form (Expected Death in the Home) if you would like to die at home. There is even a Private Transfer Permit Application form that allows family to transport their loved one"s body to the funeral home or crematorium. This may seem strange, but it allows families to gather and have more time with the deceased, perhaps to have a wake or some other kind of ritual.

After 75 years of age, it is assumed that, no matter your apparent health, you are more vulnerable and less resilient to disease and injury. Advance Care Planning should be an urgent call to action at this point in your life.
