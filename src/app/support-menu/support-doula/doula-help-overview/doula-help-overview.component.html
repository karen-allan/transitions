Help With the Hard Stuff

You may have never heard of an End of Life Doula. There are Doulas that help with pregnancy and childbirth - they are called Birth Doulas. There are also Doulas that are there for you after you are all grown up! Contrary to what it sounds like an End of Life Doula isn't just there to help you die. We provide many other services - in fact Palliative Care suggests that everyone over 19 years of age should have completed some kind of end of life contingency plan.

The truth we can't run away from is that some day we all leave this earth. What you choose to leave behind could be either a huge mess for family to sort or a well planned legacy that your family will benefit from and appreciate. Doing a care plan isn't just important for their sake but for your own as well. Because if you end up at the hospital some day without any kind of thought to this, your future could end having it decided by a stranger who probably really wished he or she didn't have to make the hard decisions.

Not everyone needs to consider their contingency plan to the same degree. Someone who is 39 may want very different medical interventions and care than someone who is 89. DWDC__2021ACPkit.docx) reproduced here; can help you decide the kind of questions you should be considering depending on which category you are in:
  • ☯  You are under 65 and in good health and fitness.
    Serious illness and injury can happen at any age. You likely will want any and all treatment for conditions that develop, but it’s not too early to think about what your values, beliefs and preferences for care would be if you were in a serious accident, you had a major stroke, or developed any other condition that might render you incapable of making decisions for yourself, and to share your thoughts with your loved ones.
  • ☯  You have one or more chronic conditions, at any age.
    If you fit within this definition, it is crucial that you prepare a plan as soon as possible. The most common chronic health conditions are:
    • ☀ Diabetes (insulin dependent or non-insulin dependent) – even if well-controlled
    • ☀ High blood pressure (hypertension) – even if well-controlled
    • ☀ Kidney disease (your health care provider may or may not use the word ‘failure’)
    • ☀ Heart disease: Abnormal Rhythm, Angina, Heart Attack, Congestive Heart Failure, Cardiomyopathy
    • ☀ Lung disease (COPD or other)
    • ☀ Liver disease
    • ☀ Neurological Diseases such as Parkinson’s, ALS, Huntington’s, and Multiple Sclerosis
    • ☀ Vascular disease
    • ☀ Autoimmune disease
    • ☀ Early to moderate dementia
  • ☯  You have a life-threatening illness and your life-expectancy is less than one to two years, at any age:
    Receiving a life-threatening diagnosis can be destabilizing, but you can still take control of your own end-of-life plans. The choices are yours to make. Think about doing your Advance Care Planning early in your diagnosis so it is done, and then revisit and revise your Advance Directive as your health changes.

  • ☯  You are over 65 and relatively healthy
    Your body is getting older, a little less resilient and progressively more fragile. As we age, it is important to think a little deeper and more often about what we do and do not want should we become seriously ill or injured. It also becomes more important to talk to those who will be making health care decisions for you if you become unable to do so for yourself so that they are not blindsided by any sudden health changes and decisions they may have to make, and are prepared by knowing what you want.

  • ☯  You are over 65 and having trouble managing your day-to-day affairs and the activities of daily living.
    This is referred to as a “frail elder” We all age differently. Mild frailty occurs when people need help with their day-to-day care and affairs, such as meal preparation and housework. As people become increasingly frail, it becomes more and more important to think about Advance Care Planning.

  • ☯  You are over 75, no matter your state of health
    This is considered the age where everyone, no matter their apparent health, is more vulnerable and less resilient to disease and injury. Advance Care Planning should be an urgent call to action at this point in your life.
When I ask these questions to clients I am often surprised at the different responses I get. Some people are okay with giving control over to others while other clients would find that to be their worst nightmare. Other questions to consider are what medical interventions would you want to receive if any, should it mean you lose some of your mental or physical faculties?

How Do I Control My Own Fate?

You may have never heard of a form called a Temporary Substitute Decision Maker List (TSDM) but it could end up being one of the biggest contributors to your future health.
If you are in an accident or suffer a critical illness and end up in Emergency and are incapable of answering for yourself, the first thing the hospital will look for is information about your next of kin. They will start at the top of the TSDM list and if that first person is not qualified or available to advocate for you, they will then move down the list. By law they must go by the hierarchy set out on this form.

To qualify as a TSDM, the people placed on the list must:
  • ♦ be 19 or over
  • ♦ be mentally capable of making decisions
  • ♦ have no dispute with you
  • ♦ have been in contact with you within the year before you need care

Why Should I Care About This?

Let's say you are separated from your spouse but not yet divorced. Maybe you now have a new love in your life, who at this point has no legal association with you. Should you get in the situation described above, where you have lost your ability to advocate for yourself, they will contact the first person on the list, your ex. They will not even consider your new person as they are nowhere on this list. If the idea of this scares you, then know that you have a say in ensuring your wishes for care are known. Setting up your own Substitute Decision Maker keeps you in control of your own life.

1. Personal Reflections: Hopes + Fears

What hopes do you have in the event that you are in the hospital requiring major treatment?
What fears do you have in the same situation?

2. Personal Reflections: Lived Experience

Have you ever had to make medical decisions for someone?
What was that experience like?
What would you have wanted to know to make the situation more supported?
Have you personally experienced a significant medical event?
What did you take away from that experience?
What helped with the situation?
What were things that made the situation more difficult?

3. Personal Reflections: Pain

Have you personally experienced persistent pain?
What did you take away from the experience?
What were things that helped ease your suffering?
Did anything make the experience more difficult for you?
How important is pain management for you?
Are you willing to be foggy-headed or sleeping most of the time if you are given strong pain meds?

4. Personal Reflections: Personal Care

How important is cleanliness to you?
What is important to you with respect to personal care?
Are you okay with family members providing personal care for you (bathing, toileting)?
Would you prefer professional care providers to tend to your needs?

5. Personal Reflections: Mortality

How much are you willing to go through for more time?
What does that time look like?
At what point would you want to be comfort-based and not cure-based?

6. Personal Reflections: Independence

How important is it for you to live independently
Would you be comfortable living in an assisted living facility?

7. Personal Reflections: Dignity

In the context of health and medical care, what does dignity mean to you?
How important is dignity to you?

8. Personal Reflections: Spirituality

Are there any spiritual or religious practices that bring you comfort, or that you would like if you were seriously ill or at end of life?

9. Personal Reflections: Final Days

If possible and reasonable, where would you like to die?
At home, in a hospital, in a hospice facility?

10. Personal Reflections: Natural Death

Allow natural death is when death is near and you receive medically appropriate care for symptoms such as pain or shortness of breath but would not have any treatment or procedure which would delay the moment of death.
What do you think about this? What does this bring up for you?
