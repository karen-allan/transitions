Who do you most trust with your life?

Let's say you are in an accident or suffer a critical illness and end up in Emergency and are incapable of answering for yourself. When you arrive at the hospital - a health care team member is legally required to search for your next-of-kin in order to find an advocate for you. They must then follow the directives set by law called many different names depending on the province you are in, but in general looks something like the one that B.C. uses called the Temporary Substitute Decision Maker List (TSDM).

The health care provider will start at the top of this form contacting a next of kin. If the first person on that list is not qualified or available to advocate for you, they will then move down the list. The hierarchy of contacts set out on this form is:

  • Spouse
  • Adult Children if any
  • Parents
  • Siblings
  • Grandparents
  • Grandchildren
  • Others Related by Birth or Adoption
  • Close Friend(s) and Others related by marriage

If you have no issues with the idea that your health and life decisions are about to be made by a family member or friend selected by a health care professional you have never met, then this might work for you. If, however you know that you want your oldest grand-child who is legally eligible to be the decision maker for you, but you have not written this wish down, it is highly unlikely that they will work their way down the list to that level. Maybe instead, they will choose your spouse, the top person on the list to call. This could be a soon-to-be-ex-spouse who may not have your best interests at heart. Your new significant other, who is your true lover, may not even show up on the list.

It is for this reason, that it is smart and proactive to designate your own preferred Health Care Advocate who will, in most regions of Canada, be able to make those life and death decisions on your behalf. By simply stating your Advocate, you can quickly and easily select the one or, at maximum two people you trust who would best handle any health related decisions if you are not in a position to do so due to accident or injury. You are letting the health care team know who you have specifically chosen to make decisions for you related to your situation.

Your Formal Substitute Decision Maker must follow the directives you have laid out in this Enhanced Representation Agreement should you become incapacitated. If you are only partially ‘incapable’ you can still make some decisions and these will be given priority. There is no need to involve a Lawyer, Doctor or Notary Public as Representative 9 is legal as long as proper witnesses and signatures are made.

Personal decisions include where you live (examples are a hospital, home care, hospice etc.), your diet, clothing and hygiene and activities. It also includes decisions about who can have contact with you.

Health care decisions include tests, examinations, treatments and procedures related to your health such as surgery, medication and vaccines. It also gives permission for the Representative to make decisions about life support and life prolonging procedures. It does NOT include decisions related to financial or legal decisions. If you are still of sound mind, you will always be the first to make your own decisions. However, if not, the health care provider will adhere to your fully legal Section-9 Representative document.

There are a number of forms created by different agencies including the Attorney General of B.C., the Dying with Dignity Association and Palliative Care B.C. They may vary wording slightly but they must all follow the legal guidelines. You can view and download the Representative Section-9 form here from the B.C. government website.

Ask your Representatives first and make sure they are willing to take on the task. Your witnesses do not need to be present during the signing with the Representatives, but they do need to see you sign the form.
