∞ You are under 65, with good health, no medical issues and feel your fitness level is good:
All adults are encouraged to put a basic health care plan together in case of an accident or emergency. This plan should include who you would like to make decisions for you should you be incapacitated in some way. Generally if you are young and healthy you will want them to agree to any and all life saving treatments.

This is something most of us never think of. But in most provinces in Canada, if you haven't got any kind of document written that designates your primary and/or secondary Advocate, this choice for who will be contacted on your behalf will be made by a health care professional you have likely never met.

Let's say you are in a car accident and unable to speak for yourself. When you arrive at the hospital - a health care team member is legally required to search for your next-of-kin and then fill out a list called many different names depending on the province you are in, but in general looks something like this one that B.C. uses (Temporary Substitute Decision Maker):
  • Spouse
  • Adult Children if any
  • Parents
  • Siblings
  • Grandparents
  • Grandchildren
  • Others Related by Birth or Adoption
  • Close Friend(s) and Others related by marriage
If you have no issues with the idea that your health and life decisions are about to be made by a family member or friend selected by a stranger, then this might work for you. If, however you know, for instance, that you want your oldest grand-child who is legally eligible to be the decision maker for you, but you have not written this wish down, it is highly unlikely that they will work their way down the list to that level. Maybe instead, they will choose your spouse, the top person on the list to call. This could be a soon-to-be-ex-spouse who may not have your best interests at heart. Your new girlfriend, who is your true love, may not even show up on the list.

It is for this reason, that it is smart and proactive to designate your own preferred Health Care Advocate who will, in most regions of Canada, be able to make those life and death decisions on your behalf. By simply stating your Advocate, you can quickly and easily select the one or, at maximum two people you trust who would best handle any health related decisions if you are not in a position to do so due to accident or injury. You are letting the health care team know who you have specifically chosen to make decisions for you related to your situation.
∞ You suffer from one or more serious, chronic medical conditions, at any age:
No matter what your age, if you have any of the chronic health conditions you need to be more prepared for a health crisis. Chronic health conditions to consider include:
  • Insulin Dependent or Non-Insulin Dependent Diabetes
  • Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)
  • Heart Diseases such as Angina, Congestive Heart Failure, Heart Attack or Enlarged Heart
  • Lung Disease such as COPD, Pulmonary Edema, Pulmonary Fibrosis, Pneumonitis
  • Liver Disease such as Cirrhosis, Hepatitis B or C
  • Parkinson’s, ALS, Huntington's or Multiple Sclerosis
  • Vascular Diseases such as Stroke, Aneurysms, Peripheral Artery Diseases, Hypertension
  • Auto-immune Disorders such as Addisons, Graves, Hashimotos or Graves Disease
  • Dementia such as Alzheimer's, Huntingtons or Creutzfeldt-Jakob Disease
If you have these or any other serious, chronic condition it is very important to put a plan together for your family to follow. In addition to selecting the person or persons who will Advocate for you in the event of an emergency, you should also create an Advanced Care Plan. This allows you to add more information about the kinds of specific treatments and procedures that you may want to say yes or no to.
∞ You have a life-threatening illness and your life-expectancy is less than one to two years, at any age:
If you have been told some kind of time limit to your life, it is imperative to make an end of life plan. The plan can be as simple or as detailed as you like and can include Do Not Resuscitate, No CPR orders, or plans for an EDITH (Expected Death in the Home), funeral arrangements, celebrations etc.

As with the other situations shown here, for your own health and well-being, you need to declare one or two people who you trust would Advocate for you in the event you can't do this for yourself. If you want to ensure that your needs and wishes are respected right to the end, you should also seriously consider making an Advanced Care Plan. This allows you to consider a myriad of questions and concerns as to how you wish to be treated medically.
∞ You are over 65 and relatively healthy:
Even if you are still healthy and feeling fine, getting your affairs in order at this age, is essential to your peace of mind. Even if your family doesn't want to discuss end-of-life planning, they will thank you for preparing for the inevitable. You probably have a Will in place and maybe even have a life insurance policy, so by completing an Advance Care Plan and by choosing a Health Care Advocate you and your family will be well prepared.
∞ You are over 65, still independent but are having trouble managing your day-to-day affairs and activities:
If you are a “frail elder” meaning you are still of sound mind and health but you do need help with your daily care, meals and house-keeping then it is even more essential to think ahead. Your family may be pressuring you to move into a care home, but you may wish, as most do, to stay at home till the very end. In that case you should know about E.D.I.T.H. (Expected Death in the Home) that allows you to ask to die at home when the time comes.

Even though you are getting by with help from home care aids and other services, you need to protect yourself by choosing an Advocate, by making an advanced care plan. These forms, placed safely away with their whereabouts known to family and your doctor will allow medical personnel to know you wishes.
∞ You are over 75, no matter your state of health:
As someone over 75, no matter what your state of health, getting your plans in order is something you just need to do. You should have an an advanced care Plan in place for your family and the medical team to follow. Choosing who you want as your health care advocate is also essential.

In this situation along with the above you may want to fill out a DNR (Do Not Resuscitate) or No CPR form. This tells Paramedics and Hospital staff that you do not wish to be revived through Artificial Respiration methods.

You may have made the decision that you would like to die at home when the time comes. In that case you will want to fill out an E.D.I.T.H. form (Expected Death in the Home) form so your wishes can be known to family and the medical team.
