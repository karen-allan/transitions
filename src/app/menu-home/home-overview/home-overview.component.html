Facing the Facts

In 1920–1922 life expectancy in Canada for men in was 58.8 years, for women it was 60.6 years.
1
 In 2022, life expectancy for men in Canada was 80.9 years, for women it was 84.7 years.
2
 In 1900, the top causes of death were cardiovascular and renal disease, pneumonia, flu, bronchitis and tuberculosis.
3
 People were cared for by family and died at home after which neighbors and friends would visit to pay their respects.
4
 With advances in hygiene, sanitation methods, antibiotics and vaccines, people began to put their faith in science and medicine.
4
 By 1950, patients were being sent to hospitals instead of staying at home, even though over 50% of them died there anyway.
4
 When a doctor couldn't save them they were relegated to the care of nurses who never received training in the death process.
4
 With strict visiting rules up until the year 2000, including a ban of children visiting at all, many patients died alone.
4
 Today, about 10% of us die of sudden death, 20% from an acute illness while 70% will die from an often un-treatable chronic illness.
4
 Most beleive they can receive Palliative Care but in 2007 the govt. discontinued the palliative and end-of-life care strategy.
4
 The Canadian Hospice Palliative Care Association finds that only 16% to 30% of Canadians receive any form of palliative care.
5
 Under the Canada Health Act, home care is not an essential service and is relegated to each province to set their own standards.
6
 Most patients have to rely on family caregivers who devote about 54 hours a week for care and must pay at least 25% of the cost.
4
 70% of patients requested comfort measures only (no life-prolonging treatments) but 54% of these patients were admitted to ICU.
5
 70% of us will die in hospitals often after receiving “pointless, often stressful, heroic measures to prevent death.
4

5:The Canadian Hospice Palliative Care Association Fact Sheet - updated 2014
